situation: |
  With the accessibility remediation well past halfway, new pain points emerged around learner engagement and data collection design. Formal data collection had never gone beyond a single end-of-course Survey Monkey survey, deployed at the beginning of the last week of class — a legacy tool with little applicability across the partner portfolio and invisible to all partners simultaneously. Canvas's built-in tools, Rubrics and Course Analytics, had never been enabled and configured to deliver consistent results. And the program had never had an overarching multimedia strategy since inception — the last attempt predated the current LMS by more than a decade.
constraint: |
  The politics of ownership made the survey initiative the hardest to navigate. The partner brandished institutionally-licensed Qualtrics as an alternative to Survey Monkey, sparking internal debate about giving up primary responsibility for Course Evaluation Surveys that fed both Learning Design and Student Support Services. Meanwhile, Canvas was evolving faster than faculty could adapt — Instructure completely redeveloped Canvas Rubrics mid-flight, with students reporting more grading errors and faculty reporting more grade inflation than before. And the multimedia that survived the accessibility remediation was better formatted for students but instrumented for nothing: no start/stop data, no click-throughs, no assessment.
decision: |
  Three forks, each named. First, I gave the partner the green light to rebuild the surveys in Qualtrics — owning the data-collection tool was not critical; staying in the data loop and improving how data was organized and delivered was. Releasing that task freed multiple departments stretched beyond contractual obligation, and for the first time practitioner instructors could see more than a top-line number, lead faculty could perceive cohort performance, and revising faculty could discuss each term's results. Second, I enabled Rubrics as a Canvas menu item for faculty and student visibility and reconfigured grading on a balanced weighted scale with every assignment graded out of 100 points. Program administrators likened it to "a change in currency," but the standard held because of the clarity it provides between faculty and students — and it surfaced grading patterns across courses that had never been visible. Third, I stapled multimedia instrumentation to the accessibility remediation (Case 4): working with the team's Data Scientist, I pushed learning designers to build check-your-understanding questions into every applicable multimedia piece — no fewer than one, no more than two — to feed data points into course- and program-level dashboards.
artifact: |
  Qualtrics: 33 courses unified into 1 reporting layer; evaluation forms generated by term (six per year), course type (content vs. internship/practicum), and faculty cohort; a 26-question instrument covering navigation, workload, communication, objectives, media effectiveness, tools, workplace applicability, and instructor performance; deployed one week before term end with no closing date. Balanced Gradebook: 28 courses converted to one reporting format — all assignments out of 100 points, rubrics aligned to the department grading schema, weighted individually or by group. Multimedia: 252+ pieces (158 interactive, 94 video-based); check-your-understanding coverage grew from 8 courses and 22 pieces in year one to 19 courses and 57+ pieces in year two; course- and program-level dashboards approved in Q2 2026.
measuredOutcome: |
  Data sources unified from 33 courses to 1 survey reporting layer, 28 courses to 1 grading format, and 19 courses to 1 multimedia reporting layer. Reporting cadence moved from once-per-term to continuous. CYU coverage more than doubled year-over-year (22 → 57+ pieces; 8 → 19 courses), and 36% of interactive pieces now carry embedded assessment. Most of this infrastructuralization has succeeded to date, but parts have stalled — none more stark than the pipeline from Canvas Course Analytics directly to Learning Designers, whose evaluation data still pipes exclusively to the partner.
engineeringTranslation: |
  Re-architected a fragmented learning-data stack into a unified, instrumented reporting layer — negotiated data-system ownership to keep the data flowing rather than holding the tool, standardized rubric and grading schemas so cross-course analytics became comparable, and built event collection for engagement and assessment directly into the multimedia layer to feed course- and program-level dashboards. Schema standardization, instrumentation, and build-vs-own tradeoffs on a live production system.
