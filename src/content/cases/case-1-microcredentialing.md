situation: |
  My first full-time instructional design role put me on-site at an online college people called "the 100-year-old startup" — an institution that had reinvented itself repeatedly and kept a relentless appetite for experimentation. Already embedded in community-college and corporate upskilling, the college was weighing a sharper strategic question: with more upskilling moving in-house through Coursera and edX, should we compete differently? Microcredentialing and learning pathways were the bet.
constraint: |
  Two pressures at once. Our partnership with the Education Design Lab sat outside their official pilot, so we couldn't copy-paste their infrastructure — we had to design our own implementation. And an existential clock was running: a wave of potential mergers and buyouts threatened to derail the project before it could prove itself.
decision: |
  The college sent me to the 2018 Summit on the Credentialing Economy. I came back with a strategy — Partnership, Platform, Pilot Testing — and a working group with the Education Design Lab out of Washington, D.C. After workshopping it with internal leadership, we chose not to enter a formal partnership; instead we applied learning-pathways logic to existing stakeholder needs, mapped a set of 21st-century-skills badges, and ran the proof of concept against complementary vendors (Credly, Accredible, Portfolium). By mid-2019 we were pilot-testing badges in Canvas, ready to deploy to students that fall.
artifact: |
  Badge taxonomy (8 skills): Initiative, Collaboration, Creative Problem-Solving, Critical Thinking, Intercultural Fluency, Empathy, Oral Communication, Resilience. Mapped across learning domains with scaffolded pathways design and custom completion criteria. Spanning three degree carousels — BBA (20 courses), MBA (25 courses), DBA (15 courses) — and designed downstream toward badged certifications and employer recognition. Timeline: May 2018 – September 2020.
measuredOutcome: |
  The honest number is zero issuances. The university hit a financial crossroads in 2019; the project was dropped and I was laid off in 2020 before any of it reached students. What I can defend is the build: a working MVP of a learning-pathways ecosystem on the leanest possible stack — Canvas, a community of practice, no vendor lock-in — backed by strong content mapping across 65 courses in three carousels. The origin story of how I think about systems.
engineeringTranslation: |
  Greenfield systems design under a closing acquisition window. Designed a badge taxonomy and completion-criteria schema, ran a build-vs-buy vendor evaluation (Credly/Accredible/Portfolium), and shipped a working pathways-ecosystem MVP on minimal infrastructure (Canvas + a community of practice, no vendor lock-in).
