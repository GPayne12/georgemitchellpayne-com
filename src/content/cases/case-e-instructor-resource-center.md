situation: |
  After acting on the program's first and only faculty survey, I turned it into a yearly recurring feedback mechanism, with targeted questions to scope upcoming development — faculty assignment awareness, planned time away, expected textbook updates, and multimedia expectations. Year one's big takeaway was the Course Revision Log (Case C). Year two's, drawn from 100+ logged entries, was faculty decentralization: Revising Faculty prepared courses, Lead Faculty oversaw live cohorts, and Practitioner Instructors graded and gave student feedback — three siloed groups who couldn't see across development, cohort prep, and instruction.
constraint: |
  Centralizing course-development knowledge drove the initiative, but three emergent questions shaped it: whether the resource center should be built as a website or a course, and how to structure maintenance; how to scope content breadth, depth, and organization for distinct audiences; and how to design the faculty permissions structure and quality-feedback mechanisms.
decision: |
  A third-party website would have been hard to operate and maintain, so I looked across the partner portfolio, found only a handful of programs running instructor resource centers, and borrowed the layout from the one already formatted to Canvas. To keep it from rotting, I wired maintenance into the recurring course-development schedule — it now updates on a yearly cycle with light touch-ups in between. The center is built on a principle: siloed faculty working at cross purposes should be able to strengthen their practice through transparent high-level documentation and shared best practices. Each faculty type gets targeted guides and calls-to-action, but all resources are open to all faculty except Program Administrator materials — letting me develop program processes with leadership "in the open" while running targeted training for every faculty type. Content is organized into: Program Administration (unpublished to faculty), Start Here, Course Development (Revising/Lead Faculty), Prior to Course Facilitation, Course Facilitation, Course Completion and Evaluation, and Course Documentation. Faculty are provisioned by active teaching cohort, so each term's faculty get the resources they need for the duration of their teaching period.
artifact: |
  Program: 33-course carousel (29 content courses, 4 practicum/internship). Faculty reach and adoption: 4 Program Admins (100%), 28 Revising/Lead Faculty (82%), 100+ Practitioner Instructors (38%). The Course Documentation section holds the current Syllabus, Instructor Guide, Course Revision Log, and Course Materials List (Leganto/Alma).
measuredOutcome: |
  Onboarded 5 new faculty through the IRC, a 25% reduction in overall onboarding time. 100+ hours of time logged across faculty types. 33% overall increase in Course Revision Log entries since the IRC went live in 2024 (see Case C), including a 56% increase in Practitioner Instructor entries. Created the opportunity to develop a Pre-Development Survey for project scoping, deployed 4 weeks before Course Development Kickoff starting in Summer 2026.
engineeringTranslation: |
  Built a role-permissioned knowledge platform with cohort-based provisioning — structured content and access by user type, wired maintenance into the existing release cycle so it wouldn't rot, and cut onboarding time 25%. Information architecture and internal tooling for a decentralized user base.
