situation: |
  The program I led at Boundless Learning had always leaned on a reliable file-synchronization system to store and distribute its course materials — much of it copyrighted, all of it living in one database. Twice in the previous two years, large cross-sections of the licensed institutional databases went unreachable when an API and permalink structure shifted underneath us. Broken links had to be fixed at the root, not patched ad hoc across courses and terms. In December 2023, the program's lead eLibrarian asked me to implement Alma/Leganto to stabilize the procurement, delivery, and maintenance of course materials, with a February 2024 target.
constraint: |
  The rollout was bound to the revision cycle: inducting course materials and aligning them across the curriculum could only move as fast as courses came up for revision, so reaching 90% of the curriculum would take at least a year. That slow cadence then collided with the integration itself. The installed LTI introduced a naming-convention error that forced nearly a thousand text changes across courses and documentation. At the course level, not all materials were copyrighted, which created misalignment both across courses and within individual ones. And later — the hardest failure — the LTI abruptly locked every Canvas parent course out of sync with its corresponding parent list, breaking the course-development workflow itself.
decision: |
  Speed of implementation was the real constraint. This was a program-standard update touching nearly every course and every person at once, but course-level work is tied to a single revision-cycle window each year — so I surfaced the structural changes during the consultation window and taught faculty to use the tool as we built it into each course. We held the rollout-and-training schedule for 82% of the first year, carving out exceptions for only six courses, almost all driven by sudden enrollment-based cancellations. As implementation gave way to maintenance, the mandate widened: Leganto made resources easy enough to reach that some revision projects mounted their entire libraries in it, and the naming-convention friction smoothed out. What remained were two integration scars — the Canvas integration that locked parent courses out of the database for months, and a post-term cleanup that still leaves dozens of orphaned lists to cull by hand, a step the partner may automate within six months. The primary objectives held: fully accessible course resources for faculty and students, and materially less time spent keeping courses accurate for everyone involved.
artifact: |
  Timeline: February 2024 – March 2026. Coverage: 334 items deployed through the tool out of 451 managed across the program. List lifecycle: review/update the parent reading list during consultation → confirm final faculty sign-off → duplicate the parent list into each section → approve Lead Faculty to review with Practitioner Instructors → course launch → remove live-section lists from the database on completion. Primary users span the full chain — Lead Learning Designer (tool administration and pre-duplication), Learning Designers (maintenance), the eLibrary (institutional licensing), Course Reserves (program- and course-level access), faculty (Revising define the list, Lead teach from it, Practitioner Instructors facilitate student access), and students. Data administration is split across the partner institution (Library, Course Reserves) and Boundless Learning (Learning Design). Data organization: Leganto/Alma holds the fully accessible materials, listed in order of first appearance with each item cited once; Canvas carries the navigation links and references (syllabus, course pages). Materials tracked — Primary Textbooks, Peer-Reviewed Articles, Licensed Video, Practitioner Templates, Department Forms — each specified by Location, URL, Citation, Documentation Type, and Required/Optional.
measuredOutcome: |
  100% of course materials fully accessible. The rollout also hardened program protocol: revising faculty finalize the Leganto list; the Lead Learning Designer duplicates it into each live-section reading list before launch; Lead faculty and Practitioner Instructors work only from the pre-approved set, using Announcements to note any changes in live sections; and the Lead Learning Designer meets Course Reserves each term to cull retired lists. Setup time saved per cycle: up to 1 hour per Practitioner Instructor per term (manual imports eliminated), up to 4 hours per Learning Designer per revision cycle (manual link checks), up to 10 hours for the Lead Learning Designer per revision cycle (program-level pre-launch review), and up to 12 hours per Course Reserves Specialist per term (coordination overhead).
engineeringTranslation: |
  A root-cause fix for link-rot, executed as a system integration rather than manual patching — migrated the program onto a centralized reference-management tool (Leganto/Alma ↔ Canvas) and defined a list-lifecycle state machine (parent list → per-section duplication → removal on completion) as its backbone. The work was as much failure-mode handling as build: reconciling a ~1,000-change LTI naming-convention migration, absorbing a copyright-driven data-alignment mismatch, and containing an integration fault that locked parent courses out of sync — with orphaned-record cleanup still queued for automation. A governed, revision-cycle-paced rollout (82% on schedule, six exceptions) carried materials to 100% accessibility while cutting recurring maintenance by up to 10–12 hours per role per cycle. Systems integration, lifecycle-state design, and operational efficiency on a live production system.
