situation: |
  Tracking course-development concerns, considerations, and requests is how instructional design stays accountable to its stakeholders. When I joined Pearson, the legacy process was to log revision notes after the fact in a Word document or spreadsheet. Promoted to Lead Instructional Designer, I inherited a directive: act on a faculty survey collected just before my predecessor left, which asked for greater transparency in the revision process and a way to record findings collectively.
constraint: |
  Time. The survey went out before my promotion, so by the time I owned the results the clock was already running. The goal was to capture substantive written feedback from faculty at the moment of concern — but where most program-wide initiatives take a year to mature, the revision log wasn't expected to produce meaningful data for at least a year after launch.
decision: |
  Within two months my team shipped a database-driven revision-log tool embedded in every course, visible only to faculty, administrators, and learning-design staff. Rolling it out course-by-course rather than handing it to a central implementation team was my call: I wanted to see how my learning designers operated with me at the boundary between front-end and back-end, so we delegated only the sweeping CSS work to the partner's information-services team.
artifact: |
  Timeline: January–September 2022. Tool location: Canvas > Modules > Instructor Resources (hidden from students). Features: Add New Request; sort by Launch Term, Last Updated, Status, Requester Name, or LD Name; filter by status (New / Complete / Deferred / Question / Pending). Table fields: Date, Location, Initial Notes, LD Notes, Status. Impact at launch: 98% reduction in lost or duplicated requests; 88% active-adoption rate among full-time faculty; 10–25+ hours saved per course revision cycle across stakeholders.
measuredOutcome: |
  Program: 33-course carousel (29 content courses, 4 practicum/internship). Faculty reach: 4 Program Admins, 28 Revising/Lead Faculty, 100+ Practitioner Instructors. Requests logged — Year 1: 112, Year 2: 146, Year 3: 176. Usage split: ~40% logged during live sections, ~30% post-completion, ~20% off-season, ~10% during active LD collaboration. Entry types: Learning design – structure (~34%), Learning design – content (~24%), Learning design – assessments (~24%), Multimedia (~12%), Typos/grammar (~2%). Created the opportunity to develop the Instructor Resource Center (Case 5).
engineeringTranslation: |
  Specced and shipped an embedded internal tool in two months — a database-backed request tracker with role-based access control and a status-workflow state machine — and chose a course-by-course rollout over central IT so the team owned the front/back-end boundary. Lost and duplicated requests dropped 98%.
